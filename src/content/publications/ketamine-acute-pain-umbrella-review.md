## Abstract

Ketamine offers effective analgesia with fewer side effects than traditional pain medications, making it a promising option for acute pain management, though its role in perioperative care warrants further study. This umbrella review searched PubMed, Scopus, and the Cochrane Library for systematic reviews on ketamine use in acute pain, extracting data on study types, comparators, dosing, administration routes, and outcomes.

Of 807 records screened, 20 systematic reviews met the inclusion criteria; per AMSTAR-2 evaluation, most were rated critically low quality. Intravenous ketamine administered perioperatively reduced pain intensity within 15–30 and 60 minutes of treatment, and decreased postoperative opioid consumption by 14–50% at both 24 and 48 hours after surgery.

The evidence suggests intravenous ketamine reduces pain intensity and postoperative opioid use while lowering the risk of nausea and vomiting, making it a valuable adjunct in perioperative pain management.
